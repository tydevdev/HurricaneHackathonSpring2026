# Quiet Collapse

## One-Sentence Pitch

Quiet Collapse is a normal, useful-looking website that slowly falls apart as the visitor interacts with it, turning ordinary browsing into a game-like act of discovery about AI overreliance, careless automation, and the hidden fragility of the web.

## Core Idea

This is the subtle variation of the concept. Unlike `slopternet/`, this version should not look like AI slop at first. It should begin as a believable, competent website: a small civic service, local resource, knowledge base, wellness tool, school portal, neighborhood guide, or product page. The first impression should be calm enough that the visitor trusts it.

The horror comes from erosion. The visitor clicks around and the site gradually loses coherence. Helpful pages become contradictory. Navigation stops matching destinations. Copy starts sounding generated. Trust signals become hollow. The site does not announce that it is broken; it lets the visitor notice.

The message is the same as Slopternet, but colder: overreliance on AI and general carelessness do not only create ugly pages. They can quietly damage the systems people rely on until the web feels unreliable by default.

## Relationship To Slopternet

These two folders explore the same warning from opposite directions:

- `slopternet/`: obviously AI-generated from the first screen, loud, satirical, instantly suspicious.
- `quiet-collapse/`: normal from the first screen, restrained, believable, increasingly creepy.

The future builds should not share the same exact UI. They can share implementation ideas, but the emotional arc should feel different.

## Inspiration Notes

Welcome Home is useful as a structural reference because it shows how an interactive website can hide unease inside friendly pages, ordinary links, page artifacts, audio, images, and close inspection.

Reference anchors:

- Official site: <https://welcomehomerestorationproject.net/>
- Overview: <https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/subcultures/welcome-home-arg>

Borrow the interaction pattern, not the creative identity:

- A normal surface that rewards investigation.
- Secrets embedded in familiar web objects.
- Hidden links, strange files, corrupted media, and tonal drift.
- Story through browsing, not cutscenes.
- A growing feeling that the page knows more than it says.

Avoid copying:

- No puppets.
- No neighborhood.
- No restoration-project premise.
- No copied characters, names, visual motifs, or lore.
- No direct imitation of the Welcome Home art style.

## Message

The warning should be felt through use:

Careless AI-generated systems can make the internet look functional while hollowing out its reliability. The most dangerous web rot may not be loud. It may look normal until people need it.

This version should make the visitor wonder how many real websites are already one bad automation layer away from this.

## Experience Arc

### Stage 1: Trustworthy Surface

The site opens as a clean, ordinary website. It should not parody AI yet.

Possible surface identities:

- A local emergency-preparedness guide.
- A school resource portal.
- A health benefits explainer.
- A small-town services page.
- A product support knowledge base.
- A community bulletin site.

The surface should have real-seeming structure:

- Search.
- Navigation.
- FAQ pages.
- Forms.
- Update dates.
- Resource cards.
- Contact links.
- A small chatbot or help assistant.

The page should feel calm, useful, and normal.

### Stage 2: Minor Inconsistencies

Early interactions reveal small issues that could be brushed off.

Examples:

- Search results answer a slightly different question.
- Update dates disagree by a few days.
- A resource card repeats a sentence.
- A help article links to the wrong section.
- The assistant says something plausible but not quite correct.
- A form field label changes after focus.
- A navigation item leads to a page with the right title but wrong content.

The visitor should feel a little uncertain, not alarmed.

### Stage 3: Reliability Failure

The page starts failing in ways that matter.

Examples:

- Important instructions contradict each other.
- The search page returns autogenerated summaries with no sources.
- The chatbot invents policy details.
- The contact form says it submitted, then shows the message still unsent.
- The same FAQ has multiple answers depending on where it is opened.
- The site begins replacing specific details with generic safer-sounding language.
- Footnotes vanish.
- Buttons still work, but the outcome is less trustworthy each time.

This is the point where the site becomes game-like. The visitor clicks around to compare versions, uncover contradictions, and find what is happening.

### Stage 4: Hidden Machinery

The visitor begins finding traces of careless automation under the normal surface.

Possible artifacts:

- Prompt fragments hidden in alt text.
- Comments like `[verify this before publishing]`.
- Generated summaries that cite pages that do not exist.
- A stale changelog that claims everything was reviewed.
- Duplicate pages with different generated answers.
- Hidden route names like `/safe-answer-v4-final-final`.
- A help widget transcript where the assistant admits it cannot tell source from summary.
- Small UI labels that drift from human copy into generated filler.

The site should feel watched by its own broken process, not by a monster.

### Stage 5: The Web Rot Is Visible

The page eventually becomes unstable enough that the visitor can no longer trust any section.

Possible end-state behaviors:

- The layout mostly remains intact, but content reliability collapses.
- Search results loop between contradictions.
- Navigation becomes a map of missing pages.
- The assistant keeps offering confident summaries that disagree with visible text.
- Critical words are replaced with generic placeholders.
- The site reveals a final readable message:

> The internet does not have to look broken to stop being reliable.

This ending should be quiet and uncomfortable, not flashy.

## Core Interactions

The visitor should discover the problem by using normal website tools.

Candidate controls:

- Search bar: begins useful, then returns plausible but false summaries.
- Help assistant: starts friendly, then overconfident, then self-contradictory.
- FAQ accordion: reveals different answers across repeated opens.
- Update log: claims human review while timestamps and content disagree.
- Contact form: appears to work but exposes hidden generated routing notes.
- Navigation: slowly routes to mismatched pages.
- Resource cards: duplicate, rewrite themselves, or lose source labels.
- Print/download button: exports a corrupted version of the page.
- Report issue button: produces a generated apology and changes nothing.

## Game-Like Shape

The play loop is investigation through normal browsing:

- The visitor clicks ordinary site controls.
- Each interaction increases a hidden instability score.
- Contradictions become clues.
- Repeated visits to the same content reveal different versions.
- Hidden artifacts unlock after enough inconsistencies are found.
- The final warning appears only after the visitor has seen the site fail in several different ways.

There should be no visible score. Progress should feel like realizing the site cannot be trusted.

## Visual Direction

The opening look should be calm and credible:

- Mostly white or neutral background.
- Quiet typography.
- Restrained color palette.
- Practical navigation.
- Clear resource cards or article layouts.
- No obvious parody on first load.

The collapse should be subtle before it becomes visible:

- Slight text replacement.
- Tiny alignment drift.
- Repeated update badges.
- Missing source links.
- Duplicated paragraphs.
- Forms that preserve stale values.
- Search summaries that blur into generic filler.
- Assistant messages that become too confident.
- Occasional flicker or layout break only late in the experience.

The site should stay readable longer than Slopternet. The fear is not that it looks ugly; the fear is that it looks fine.

## Tone

Start:

- Useful.
- Calm.
- Trustworthy.
- Plainspoken.

Middle:

- Uncertain.
- Slightly wrong.
- Too confident.
- Hard to verify.

End:

- Creepy.
- Brittle.
- Quietly damning.
- Clear about the warning.

## Future Build Approach

When this becomes a real subproject, build it as an independent Vite React app inside `quiet-collapse/`.

Likely structure:

- React state tracks instability, discovered contradictions, repeated visits, and unlocked artifacts.
- Content modules should have multiple versions so repeated interactions can reveal drift.
- Search and assistant responses should be deterministic enough to feel authored, not random.
- CSS variables can introduce subtle decay late in the experience.
- Hidden artifacts can live behind ordinary interactions instead of obvious secret buttons.
- Reduced-motion mode should keep the creeping content instability without relying on flicker.
- Mobile should preserve the investigation loop through tap-friendly search, FAQ, and navigation interactions.

Root hub integration should wait until the app has a committed `dist/` build.

## Verification Goals For The Future Build

Before calling the future website done:

- Verify desktop and phone layouts.
- Verify search, FAQ, assistant, form, navigation, and repeated-click paths.
- Verify the site feels trustworthy at first.
- Verify the degradation is discoverable without becoming random.
- Verify reduced-motion behavior.
- Verify the final warning is readable.
- Verify the root hub links only after `dist/` exists.
